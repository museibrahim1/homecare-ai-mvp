# Dairy Producer Value Plan

## Shared brand + shared processing (farms do not become brands)

**Working premise:** Individual farms should not have to build consumer brands. That is a different business (marketing, retail, compliance packaging, demand planning). What farms need is a **new branded route to market** that is not Highland-style (or equivalent regional bottler/processor), so more of the retail dollar returns to the people who produce the milk.

**Core idea:** Create one regional brand owned for producers. Farms supply milk. A small processing layer turns it into finished products. The brand sells. Farms stay farms.

---

## 1. The problem (why co-ops and big names fail producers)

| Layer | What they control | Why farm pay stays low |
|---|---|---|
| Farm | Raw milk | Price-taker; perishable; short haul radius |
| Co-op / processor / bottler | Processing, brand access, grocery slots | They need cheap input milk; efficiency gains often stay with them |
| Store | Shelf and shopper | Takes retail margin |

As long as the only sale is **commodity raw milk into someone else’s brand**, producers will not get paid like brands get paid. Negotiating with Highland-type buyers is still selling into their system.

**Constraint you set (correct):** Do not ask each farm to become “Sunrise Dairy Homestead Brand.” Most farms will not win that game. Branding still has to exist. It just has to live **above** the farms, not on every farm gate.

---

## 2. The model: Producer Brand Utility

Think of three separate jobs:

```text
1. PRODUCE     Farms milk cows. No consumer branding required.
2. PROCESS     Shared mini-plant (farmer-aligned ownership).
3. BRAND/SELL  One regional brand sells finished milk and dairy products.
```

### Who owns what

| Asset | Owner | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Cows, land, milking | Individual farms | Production excellence |
| Processing equipment | Farmer-aligned LLC / trust / co-op **new vehicle** (not the old commodity co-op) | Capture processing margin |
| Brand, packaging, retail contracts | Same vehicle (or brand subsidiary) | Own the shelf story and pricing power |
| Overflow raw milk | Optional sale to legacy processors | Safety valve only, never the main plan |

**Important:** This is not “join Highland and hope.” It is a **new branded supply chain** where the brand’s job is to pay producers a transparent formula tied to finished-product revenue, not to the Midwest commodity mailbox price alone.

Call the entity something like a **Producer Brand Utility (PBU)**:

- Farms sign supply agreements (volume, quality, antibiotics, SCC, pickup windows).
- PBU processes and sells under one brand.
- Farms never run Instagram ads or pitch Whole Foods. The PBU brand team does that.
- Farms get paid on a **published pay grid** plus optional profit share when the brand clears target margin.

---

## 3. Brand strategy (without farm-as-brand)

### What the brand is
One clean regional brand. Example positioning (names TBD):

- **Promise:** Milk from named local farms, processed locally, sold under one label.
- **Proof:** Farm list on pack/site (“from 8 family dairies within 100 miles”), not 50 fake farm illustrations.
- **Products first:** Fluid milk (gallon/half), then yogurt, then 1–2 cheeses. Not 20 SKUs.

### What the brand is not
- Not each farm’s logo on the jug.
- Not a commodity co-op rebrand that still prices like Class III.
- Not “support farmers” charity marketing with the same $1.50/gal economics underneath.

### How farms show up without becoming brands
- Optional farm codes or a rotating “Farm of the month” story on the site.
- QR on carton → map of supplying farms.
- Farms can sell at their own farm store if they want; that is optional, not required.

Shoppers buy **the brand**. Farmers supply **the brand**. That split is intentional.

### Competitive wedge vs Highland-type names
Regional bottlers win on ubiquity and price. You do not beat them on every aisle.

You win on:

1. **Traceable local supply** (real farms, real radius).
2. **Fresher pull dates** from short haul + local process.
3. **Institutional and specialty retail first** (schools, hospitals, independent grocers, CSAs, office/home delivery) before fighting for the main Highland slot at every chain.
4. **Premium but reachable** pricing, not luxury vanity dairy.

---

## 4. Simplified supply chain

### Today
```text
Farm → Hauler → Highland-type processor/bottler → Distributor → Grocer → Consumer
         (farm gets raw milk price)
```

### Target
```text
Farm → PBU pickup → Shared mini-plant → Brand sales → Buyer → Consumer
         (farm paid on brand pay grid + profit share)
```

Overflow only:
```text
Farm surplus → Legacy processor (commodity price)
```

### Radius rule
Keep the operating circle roughly **within ~100 miles** of the plant so milk quality and haul cost stay sane. Expand by adding a second plant + brand region later, not by stretching one plant across three states.

---

## 5. Processing tech (efficiency without handing power back to big manufacturers)

Use tech that makes a **small plant** viable. Do not invest in tech whose only customer is a giant processor.

| Phase | Technology | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Pilot | Legal vat / mini-dairy line (MicroDairy-class or EU mini-vat systems) | Pasteurize, bottle, yogurt, simple cheese |
| Scale | Containerized mini-plant (Risto / Mallagro-style) | Faster deploy, food-safety envelope |
| Efficiency | On-farm or at-plant **membrane concentration** (e.g. Wafilin MILCON-class) | Cut water haul; feed cheese/yogurt; lower energy |
| Later | Non-thermal assist (PEF, plasma) when commercial and regulator-ready | Shelf life / quality; not day-one bet |

**Rule:** Processing CAPEX sits in the PBU. Farms are not asked to each buy a creamery.

---

## 6. How producers make more money (the part that is not branding theater)

Branding alone does not pay farms. **Ownership of the brand + processing margin** does.

### Pay design (simple)
1. **Base pay:** Above local mailbox/commodity reference by a fixed premium (e.g. +$X/cwt) for qualifying milk.
2. **Quality bonuses:** SCC, bacteria, protein/fat components.
3. **Brand dividend:** Quarterly share of PBU profit after operating reserve (allocated by pounds shipped).
4. **Floor protection:** If brand sales miss, base premium is the commitment; dividend is variable.

### What not to do
- Do not promise “retail price minus nothing.” Processing, packaging, and sales cost real money.
- Do not recreate a co-op where management captures brand value and farms only get a thin patronage check with no transparency.
- Do not let the PBU become a classic processor that buys cheapest milk available. Supply should be **contracted member farms first**.

### Open-book principle
Member farms see: retail/wholesale revenue, COGS, haul, labor, packaging, and the farm pay pool. Trust is the product for recruiting farms.

---

## 7. Go-to-market (brand sells; farms produce)

### Beachhead (months 0–12)
1. **Institutional:** 1–2 school districts or hospital foodservice contracts for fluid milk.
2. **Independent retail:** 10–20 local grocers / natural markets.
3. **Route drop / subscription:** Weekly home or office delivery in the metro (Omaha-class city) to own the customer list.
4. **SKU limit:** Whole and 2% gallon, one yogurt. Add cheese only when fluid is stable.

### Why not start in every big-chain dairy case
Chain slotting and Highland-type incumbency will crush a new brand on day one. Win density in a smaller set of accounts, prove pay to farms, then expand.

## 8. Phased plan

### Phase 0 — Validate (2–4 weeks of work, no plant yet)
- Map 5–15 dairies inside a ~100-mile ring willing to divert **part** of their milk (not 100% on day one).
- Pull real numbers: current pay price, haul, volume, quality metrics.
- Interview 20 buyers: independent grocers, one school nutrition director, one hospital FSD. Ask willingness to switch from Highland-type supply and at what premium.
- Name/brand territory check; avoid fighting trademark wars before product exists.
- Choose entity form with counsel (LLC with farmer equity vs new marketing co-op vs trust + operating company).

**Kill criteria:** No buyer premium and no farm willingness to divert volume → stop or redesign.

### Phase 1 — Soft brand + toll or micro-batch (pilot)
- Toll-process at an existing inspected plant **or** stand up smallest legal micro-line.
- Launch brand with 1–2 SKUs into 5–10 accounts.
- Pay farms the published premium on diverted pounds.
- Prove unit economics: can brand gross margin fund premium + plant + sales?

**Kill criteria:** Cannot fund the farm premium after real COGS for 90 days.

### Phase 2 — Shared mini-plant
- Container or small plant sized to contracted member volume.
- Bring yogurt (and later one cheese) in-house.
- Add membrane concentration if cheese/haul math says yes.
- Hire thin brand ops: 1 GM/ops, 1 sales, part-time QA; not a corporate dairy org chart.

### Phase 3 — Harden the system
- Multi-year farm supply contracts.
- Second metro or second plant only after Phase 2 cash-flow positive.
- Keep legacy processors as overflow only.

## 9. Roles (so farms never have to “become the brand”)

| Role | Farms | PBU / Brand |
|---|---|---|
| Milk quality | Yes | Specs + testing |
| Consumer marketing | No | Yes |
| Packaging / labels | No | Yes |
| Retail pitches | No | Yes |
| Pricing at shelf | No | Yes |
| Processing labor | No | Yes |
| Capital for plant | Optional equity | Primary raise |
| Public face | Optional stories | Brand is the face |

Farms can invest equity in the PBU if they want upside. They should not be required to run the brand.

---

## 10. Capital sketch (order of magnitude, refine in diligence)

| Need | Rough band | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Brand launch (packaging, site, first sales) | Low tens of $k | Before heavy plant |
| Micro-creamery line | ~$25k–$150k+ | Depends on vat vs container plant |
| Working capital (milk pay, packaging) | Critical | Underestimate this and the brand dies |
| Shared plant (Phase 2) | Mid–high six figures to low seven | Size to contracted volume |
| Membrane concentration | Later add-on | After product mix needs it |

Funding stack options: farmer equity + local impact/ag lenders + pre-sold institutional volume + limited outside capital with **farmer protective provisions** (so outside money cannot flip the PBU into another cheap-milk buyer).

---

## 11. Risks and how to handle them

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Brand fails to win shelf | Start institutional + indie retail; avoid Highland head-to-head |
| Farms won’t leave current buyer | Divert 10–30% first; never demand full switch on day one |
| PBU becomes the new extractive middleman | Open books, farmer board seats, pay formula in the operating agreement |
| Food safety / inspection | Hire QA early; use proven legal equipment; no cowboy processing |
| SKU creep | Cap SKUs until fluid milk pays |
| Precision fermentation / alt dairy long-term | Compete on local freshness and real farms; do not race commodity protein price |

## 12. Decisions to make next

1. **Geography:** Confirm Omaha-area (or other) radius and which farms to approach first.
2. **Entity:** Farmer-majority ownership structure (non-negotiable if the goal is producer pay).
3. **Brand territory:** Working name + category (fluid-first local dairy).
4. **Pilot path:** Toll-process vs buy micro-line first.
5. **Buyer target list:** 20 names; book meetings.
6. **Pay formula v1:** Draft the premium + dividend rules in one page farmers can understand.

---

## 13. One-sentence strategy

**Build one regional dairy brand and a small shared plant that farms supply into, so producers earn from branded finished products without each farm having to become a consumer brand, and without depending on Highland-type processors to “pay more” for the same raw milk.**

---

## Appendix A — Why this is different from a classic co-op

| Classic commodity co-op | Producer Brand Utility |
|---|---|
| Sells mostly into commodity / private label / big brand systems | Sells its **own** brand to buyers |
| Scale logic favors huge plants | Scale logic favors local density |
| Patronage often opaque | Open-book pay grid |
| Farm identity erased | Farms credited as supply base, brand still singular |
| Incentive: keep plant full cheaply | Incentive: grow brand margin and share it |

## Appendix B — Tech shortlist (for Phase 1–2 shopping)

1. MicroDairy Designs–class vat systems (US, low CAPEX entry).
2. Automated mini-dairy vats (e.g. Frigojollinox CASARO / Inventagri POLYFOOD class) for less labor.
3. Container mini-plants for turnkey inspection envelope.
4. Wafilin MILCON–class membrane concentration when volume and cheese mix justify it.
5. Park PEF/plasma as Phase 3+ watchlist.
